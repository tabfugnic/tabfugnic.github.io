Driving in Taiwan is, I hate to stereotype, like many other East Asian countries is frightening. Taiwan has very little restriction on how people drive and more just offer suggestions that are never enforced. These simple restrictions are things like not running a red light... and well that's about it. The only other one that is enforced is speeding, but only because there are cameras setup around the entire country to catch people speeding. As a result you find that many people install sensors to just be able to slow down when one is approaching. There is one more restriction that people have, they need to wear a seatbelt in the front seat on the highway. That's right, they are not required to wear it off the highway and they're never required to wear it in the back seat and believe me no does wear them. Seldom do people even bother to wear them in the front seat even on the highway.

Now keep in mind, there really is any other restriction. What lane you're in doesn't really matter. Even if the lane is designed to be a turning lane, plenty people will just skip ahead and use that to cut in line. People will get into the oncoming lane whenever they feel like if they think it will get them past the person that is in front of them, not concerned with oncoming traffic of course. There is no law saying that you must yield to pedestrians, though you will get in trouble if you hit someone on the road. People will just park where ever they see fit. Plenty of times I've seen a person double parked on a busy road not in the car at all no where to be seen. This is illegal, but the police here just don't care or don't take the time to really enforce it. I saw one where the police officer gently tapped on the window and, more or less, encouraged the person to leave the spot they were doubled parked in. Mind you, no driver was present.

Terminology that is equivalent to cutting someone off or tailgating just doesn't exist here, because it's just how people drive. Scooters, which everyone here and I mean everyone here owns, weave in and out of traffic going from one lane to the next. Going between cars and even using the sidewalk, which by the way scared the crap out of me one time walking down the street. These scooters act just like cars to, people will haul just about anything on them. This includes four people at a time, dogs that rest where the feet go, ladders, and even, most frighteningly of all, giant propane tanks just hanging off the back.

The whole philosophy associated with driving is odd. Having only been able to experience a small amount including public transportation I can't speak for everyone. Bus drivers, unfortunately drive just like they would a car, breaking only when they absolutely have to, which means flinging me to the front of the bus at a bus stop. Car drivers drive exactly like the smaller scooter attempting to weave in and out of traffic, of course only as much as the car can fit between two objects. Scooters drive like they were walking through a crowd, using any small advantage they have to get in front and breaking like they can stop in half a foot.

All around it's a frightening experience, but it's not all bad. People who drive cars are rewarded every time they need to fill up on gasoline. First of all, every gas station is full service, but most amazingly you get a present at the end of your fill up. This gift usually amounts to a couple bottles of water or pack of tissue/napkins/toilet paper. I saw all three because usually those three things are wrapped into one package. Very seldom do you find soft tissue paper, or a roll of toilet paper, but more often a pack of napkins that is meant to use all of your cleansing needs.
