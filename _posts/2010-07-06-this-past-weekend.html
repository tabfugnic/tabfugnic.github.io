So far my trip here in Taiwan has been a lot of fun. There have been a few slow parts, but that's to be expected in a three month long journey. This past weekend was great and somewhat different from the rest of my time.

I was given the chance to just roam alone for a change, so I seized the opportunity. What I really wanted to do in that time, was just get a feel for the biggest city on the island, Taipei. I woke up, took a quick look at a few things I wanted to do around Taipei Main Station and head out. The train if I haven't said so before takes probably about 50 minutes to get from my home train station (Neili) to Taipei. It's a boring trip usually, I have to fight to get a seat and I get stared at by Taiwanese people. The past few times I've just been reading Moby Dick on my phone e-book app. When I finally arrived in Taipei I started my journey by checking out some local outdoor equipment stores, I learned pretty quickly that everything there wasn't any cheaper than at home, except for some killer deals on snow gear, surprisingly. Afterwards I set off to check out yet another Electronics paradise. Taipei is full of them, they have one that is actually a government owned computer megamart, it's amazing. Prices aren't anything spectacular, but I still need to do more research on the local companies that I might get the best deals on. That said, laptops from local companies tend to be more expensive here than in the US, imagine that. This place, by the way, was call the NOVA computer arcade.

After my brief shopping trip I set off on my cultural tour of the city, albeit not a very extensive tour. I hopped on the metro and went over to Chiang Kai Shek Memorial and after checking out the Theater and Concert Halls I finally saw him. Within the same hall a small show was going on where soldiers were showing off their gun twirling skills. I liked it, but they seem do this at every government owned facility. They did this at some hall near Taipei 101. About CKS, he was sitting there in much the same fashion as Lincoln at the Lincoln Memorial. Unfortunately, the two men seem to be nothing alike. They are considering removing CKS from the hall and renaming it because it is disputed the historical significance of this man.

After CKS I walked down the street to go to the 228 Memorial Museum and learn a bit about this historic event I know nothing about. First I went to lunch, I went to a place called Mos Burger, Japanese in origin if I remember correctly. To my surprise the only thing that they were out of on a burger menu was beef. I feel like it's only in East Asia I can go to a burger place and be told that all the beef is gone. After lunch I set off. On my way I came across something I didn't see on my map and to this day I'm not exactly positive what it is. A nice gentleman in broken english showed me the way to get inside. After going through a metal detector and getting some absolutely astonished looks from the people checking my bags, I found myself in front of a heavily western influenced mansion. As I said, I don't know what this place is, but I assume it was the presidential mansion that the Japanese constructed during the occupation of Taiwan. I went through the house, no English at all. The most I could do was go around and check look at the nice designs and wish I had a house like that. When I finally got to the garden, I came to a Japanese style tea house where a man in front wearing a hat that had in big letters "DORK" across the front greeted me. He explained what the tea house was about, unfortunately he did not say anything about the mansion, but at least I knew why the teahouse was there. I then set off for 228 memorial museum and after running around the memorial park for sometime l I finally came to the museum that was being renovated for some reason.

This was starting to be quite a nice walk. After all of the museum and memorial halls I decided to check out a few temples. The first one I was to go to was the Longshan temple. I suppose there isn't much to say about temples, past being temples, but they were beautiful nonetheless. Outside Longshan old men were playing a board game I was unfamiliar with and a slightly drunk man starting speaking chinese to me. Asking where I was from, and that's all I caught. A came to a few more temples and went to a small trade show, I think, where a very enthusiastic young man tried to get me to buy a chocolate covered banana. Three more temples later I went to a night market near the Longshan temple, where I may or may not have been asked if I wanted a prostitute, either that or I was asked to go and eat at a restaurant. It's hard to tell sometimes. If it was a prostitute it was cheap or at least I assume, if it was a restaurant it was expensive. She offered whatever service it might have been for about $650 NTD (approx. $20). After declining several times I got out and made my way to the Taipei 101.

I got to Taipei 101, and getting a bit nervous as I seem to do when things involve long elevator trips, I went down to the supermarket and bought myself half a liter of Taiwan Beer, sat down, and surfed the internet. I then took the trip, it happened to be about a dollar cheaper because I had a valid student ID. The fastest elevator in the world they claim, and sure enough, it was fast as hell. We climbed to the observation deck in some 39 seconds. In the process of slowing down I had some sensation of weightlessness too. It was crazy! Taipei 101 is gigantic, they have this crazy ball that hangs at the top that's supposed to prevent problems with wind. There is another one at the bottom to handle the earthquakes. The top gave me beautiful pictures of the surrounding area at night. Then came time to leave, and sure enough like any tourist area they tried to sell me stuff. The odd thing is it was clearly way out of my price range. It was all petrified coral that was over $1000 USD. I finally got down drank another liter of beer and set off for home. I got home and they tried to feed me even more!

Saturday done.

The next day Tony and Johnny came by to take me to a mountain further northeast. The most I can say is that we saw lots of wildlife and the pictures were pretty. We had a butterfly that just wouldn't stop bothering us. We kept getting lost and ended up taking the long way back. Tony not surprisingly was really sore the next day. We then went back into the nearby town, the name I do not remember, and got an assortment of mushrooms and some peaches. It was all very delicious. We checked out a nearby dam and the lake it created that supplies the water for the entire northern part of Taiwan. We then went into Dashi, walked across a huge bridge and picked up some dinner. I got to eat intestines, which were as you might expect, kinda chewy. Not very good either, but I was reassured that the food there was just not as good as it should be. Dashi is known for its Dougan, firm tofu, and we got some of that too, but that wasn't very good either. We then went to desert and that was subpar as well or at least I was being told that, it didn't seem to off to me at all. On our way back out we came to a man that was playing music on a leaf. I'm not joking, this man can play just about any song on a single leaf. The best part, is he taught us all how to do it. I could only make one bass note, but that's fine by me. How many people can play a leaf? We then came home, I was exhausted so I hit the bed quickly. And that for the most part was my weekend.
