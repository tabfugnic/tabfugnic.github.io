It's come to my attention, as I look for a job in the states, that finding work as a programmer isn't going to be easy. Having never worked as a paid programmer, only IT with some programming, I can't attest to how much these companies actually want me to know. If it were as much as they they say then I would need to drop everything I'm doing to become intimately familiar with fifteen languages I've only dabbled with just for an entry-level position.

To put this in perspective, learning a new programming language can be equated pretty well to learning a spoken language. The concepts are easy to understand, the syntax can take awhile to grasp, but it can takes years to understand the subtleties. This is quite probably a gross exaggeration that many real programmers might disagree with, but it still provides some context.

If they don't expect that much from me and think my abroad work has some merit then I feel like I still don't come close to what is expected of me. Good thing I can interview well I suppose.

No lie, I saw a job ad that says it wants fresh or soon to be graduates with 1-3 years of professional experience in such-and-such language. I don't understand that at all. Maybe it isn't too far fetched to imagine that someone going through school has also been working full time as a programmer. I feel those people probably aren't going to be looking into entry-level work.

It's a crazy world out there, and it's gonna be fun trying to get a job in this terrible market. I think the best possible business venture I can have is developing android apps, like everyone else, and try to get some experience that way. The only problem with that though, is it goes back to what I said earlier. It's a pretty saturated market. I'm pretty sure it still doesn't have an auto-tune app, a free one for that matter.

Here's where it goes out to the people I know, all maybe three that read this blog. If you know anyone you could recommend me to, I won't stop you, hell, I'd even encourage it.
