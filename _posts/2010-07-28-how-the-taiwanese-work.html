Since I've only been here a little less than two months I am by no means an authority on the subject, but I thought I should talk about how Taiwanese work and what my typical day involves.

Let me start by covering what I do in a typical day. Every morning I wake up between 5:30AM - 6:30AM depending upon whether or not I exercise that morning. I walk upstairs into another bedroom, go out to an enclosed deck and turn on the hot water to take a shower. It's sort of an awkward experience, the shower head isn't suspended to the wall and the shower itself is surrounded by windows that looking out to tall townhouses that can without a doubt look in.

If I have enough time in the morning I run over to a local shop and buy two sandwiches for breakfast. Each one seems to be obligated to have cucumbers, egg and some sort of meat associated with it. I then sit on the couch waiting for Tony and Amily to come and pick me up and set out for work. Tony is crazy, he drives us all the way to work only to turn around and drive back into Taipei. The drive to work is at least an hour, often times about an hour and a half. It's a long drive, and it not exactly a normal commute to work here. I've said to people at work where I live here in Taiwan and they always respond the same way. "Oh! That is so very long away from here."

Then work is work. I am given small, usually ridiculous tasks, with no deadline and no expectation of completeness or quality. That's not important though. The people of Taiwan, at least where I've had the chance to experience it, take the term "the daily grind" to a whole new level. The time that you get into work isn't very important. People will filter in sometime around 9AM while others won't get until sometime after 10 o'clock.

Work is just as you might expect any other company in the States, some people work, while others will be talking or looking at something personal on their computer. This is until 12:01PM, lunchtime. Lunchtime is a big event, not in the respect that there's a lot going on, but more to do with how people treat lunchtime. From sometime around noon until almost 1:30PM the entire company is dead and the company makes an effort to make it that way. The lights are turned out and sleepy time music gets played over the intercom. Many people will eat really quickly and take long naps, while others, which is what I'm more familiar with, will grab lunch in large groups and eat together. The only thing that isn't done is work. It doesn't seem to matter how much work you have or what deadlines you need to keep, a Taiwanese lunch is used to unwind. Even if that means staying later, lunchtime and to a lesser extent dinner time is the no-work time.

After lunch it's back to the same thing as before, I continue my work and everyone else continues theirs. Some working until 6pm, but most will spend several extra hours working. For no rhyme or reason each of these people will work as late 9 - 10pm on a normal workday. This gets even worse if they have an approaching deadline. To this day I can't quite fathom why everyone works so late, they don't seem to be getting that much more work done.

When I finally leave, it's around 7pm, I walk over to the train station and if I have my climbing shoes with me, I'll go into the city and climb. That is another story all together.

When I finally get off the train where I live I take about a twenty minute walk home from the train station and walk in the door around 9pm if I do nothing else that day, but usually I get in around 11pm. Then Nainai makes sure I eat and after I eat, I go to bed.
